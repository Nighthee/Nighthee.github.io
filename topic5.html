Wolverines

One researcher named Blomqvist (2001) found that wolverines may require a higher level of thiamine (vitamin B) compared to other mustelids. This enzyme is involved in metabolizing carbohydrates and assists in the utilization of proteins and fats [1] [19]. All plants and animals have thiamine; however there are higher levels of thiamine in nuts, seeds, seafood, and whole grain products [19]. Thus, wolverines may need a higher requirement of this vitamin because the only way they obtain this micronutrient is from eating berries in summer and spring or by eating other animals which have a store of thiamine already present.
Furthermore, some wolverines have learned to eat porcupines, however in the process, many wolverines have been found dead due to the porcupine spines impaling vital organs within the digestive tract [24]. The wolverine has not developed any physiological adaptations to increase their survivability against these prey, but they may have adapted some behavioural adaptations to either avoid such prey or learn to avoid the spines when eating [24].

Giant Otter

Generally, piscivores consume the entire fish including bone, viscera, skin, and scales. Whole fish are relatively good sources of nutrients especially the mineral selenium. Sometimes animals are at risk of selenium toxicity when eating large amounts of fish [3]. Captive giant otters may have problems with vitamin E and thiamine deficiencies due to the degradation of these vitamins being very quick in killed fish [3].

Some otters have been found to have vitamin A deficiency which will affect retinal growth (dysplasia). This deficiency occurs when vitamin A is deficient over long periods of time. Vitamin A is found in many foods, but large quantities are mainly found in green vegetables in which otters do not eat [24] [34].
