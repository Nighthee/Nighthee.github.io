Wolverine and Giant otter

Both the wolverine and giant otter are part of the mustelidae family, thus, their GI tracts are very similar to those within the family such as the mink [20][38]. The diagram below is the closest representation of the wolverine and giant otter gastrointestinal physiology. Generally, the features of both these animals are the same with a mouth producing saliva for digestion and saliva is also used for heat loss [20] [3]. The esophagus involves peristalsis contractions which move food bolus down to the stomach and sphincters within this organ prevent reflux [20] [38]. Following after the esophagus is the simple stomach which acts to digest nutrients with the use of contractions and acid. The small intestine is the primary site of nutrient absorption with villi structures on the intestinal lining which increase the surface area of absorption. The large intestine reabsorbs any water and electrolytes if it is needed such as if the animal is famished or dehydrated. The wolverine does not have a caecum [38]; however the giant otter does have a very small caecum due to their carnivorous diet. Lastly, both animals have a colon and rectum which moves the feces through peristaltic contractions before they are expelled out the body through the rectum [20][38].


Transit time

Transit time is the time it takes for a marker or prey item to first appear in the faeces. Both giant otters and wolverines tend to have shorter transit times due to their high metabolic rates [3][7].
